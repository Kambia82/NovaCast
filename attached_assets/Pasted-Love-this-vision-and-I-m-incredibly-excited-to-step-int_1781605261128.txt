Love this vision, and I'm incredibly excited to step into the lead product strategist and architect role with you. Novacast has a fantastic foundation, and expanding it to bridge the gap between artificial lures and live bait is a brilliant evolution—if done right.
​The core challenge here is avoiding "feature creep" that paralyzes a beginner. If a newbie opens the app and is immediately bombarded with choices between a Texas-rigged plastic worm and the nuances of keeping minnows alive in a baitwell, they’ll close the app.
​To keep the UI radically simple while integrating both worlds, we need to focus on the user's intent rather than the category of the bait. Beginners usually ask one core question: "What do I put on my hook right now to catch a fish?"
​Here are three distinct, natural ways we can structure the user journey to integrate both live bait and artificial lures without breaking the clean, beginner-friendly UI:
​1. The "Water Conditions First" Journey (The Equalizers)
​Instead of forcing the user to choose "Lures" or "Live Bait" on the home screen, we make the entry point environmental (e.g., Water Clarity, Temperature, Target Fish).
​How it looks in the UI: The user selects their target (e.g., Bass) and current water clarity (e.g., Murky/Muddy).
​The Integration: The results page presents a unified "Top Recommendations" list that gracefully blends both worlds based on the conditions.
​Example: For muddy water, the app suggests a Vibrating Chartreuse Spinnerbait (artificial) OR a Live Nightcrawler on a split-shot rig (live), explaining why both work in low visibility (vibration and scent). They are presented as peer options, not competing systems.
​2. The "Tackle Box vs. Bait Shop" Toggle (The Mental Model)
​We mirror the real-world behavioral shift. Anglers either look at what they already own (Tackle Box) or stop to buy something fresh before a trip (Bait Shop).
​How it looks in the UI: A clean, dual-tab or toggle interface at the top of the recommendation engine: [Artificial Lures] | [Live Bait].
​The Integration: By separating them into two clean views within the same ecosystem, a beginner can explore one at a time. If they are using a baitcaster and want to practice casting artificials, they stay on the Lure tab. If they are heading out with a basic spinning rod and a bucket, they flip to the Live Bait tab to see how to hook a minnow (e.g., lip-hooked vs. dorsal-hooked) for the specific species they want.
​3. The "Stepping Stone" Progression (The Educational Journey)
​Many beginners start with live bait because it's highly effective, and then transition to artificial lures as they gain confidence. We can lean into this natural learning curve.
​How it looks in the UI: A "Style of Fishing" onboarding preference or filter: Simple & Relaxed (Live Bait) vs. Active & Searching (Artificial Lures).
​The Integration: The app treats live bait as the baseline for catching fish, but uses it to teach the fundamental concepts of artificial lures. For instance, if the app recommends a live crawfish for rock piles, it can include a subtle "Upgrade to Artificial" tip: "Enjoying this? Next time, try a plastic jig to mimic this exact movement!"
